## 8. AAHOA — 12 Points of Fair Franchising & PIP advocacy

**Verdict: Partially verified — PIPs are NOT explicitly named among the 12 Points, but PIP-adjacent advocacy and legislation are active in 2025-2026.**

- Source (fetched directly): [AAHOA — 12 Points of Fair Franchising](https://www.aahoa.com/advocacy/franchise-relations/points-of-fair-franchising). The 12 points cover termination rights, encroachment/impact protection, minimum performance guarantees, QA inspections/guest surveys, vendor exclusivity/rebates, fee transparency, relationship-building, dispute resolution, venue/choice-of-law, franchise sales ethics, transferability, and (updated at AAHOACON24) sale/change-of-control protections. **None of the 12 points explicitly names "Property Improvement Plans" or capital expenditure mandates as a distinct point** — closest adjacent points are #3 (Minimum Performance and Quality Guarantees) and #4 (QA Inspections), which are the mechanisms that typically trigger PIPs.
- Source (fetched directly): [AAHOA 2025 Policy Priorities](https://aahoa.com/advocacy/government-affairs/policy-priorities) — the visible page text emphasizes tax reform, SBA lending (LIONS Act), short-term-rental/OTA competition, tourism tax issues, and human-trafficking prevention. **PIPs are not mentioned in the visible priorities text** (a downloadable 2025 Policy Handbook may have more detail, but I could not access its contents).
- However, general search turned up a **2021 CoStar article, "AAHOA Members Voice PIP Concerns"** (WebFetch of costar.com blocked with 403, so I only have the headline/search snippet, not full text) indicating this has been a live grievance topic historically; and a search-engine synthesis (not a directly fetched primary source) stating AAHOA members "face relentless pressure on margins from PIP requirements that don't pencil out" alongside swipe fees and OTA commissions — **flag this framing as [unclear/synthesized by search tool], not a confirmed direct AAHOA quote**.
- **State legislation — this is the strongest, most current, most concrete finding on this front:**
  - **New Jersey — "Hotel Franchisee Fairness and Market Access Act"** (A5050/S4202 filed for the 2026 session): would explicitly restrict franchisors from mandating "material capital expenditure, renovation, improvement, equipment replacement, or relocation" **unless** (1) it's necessary for health/safety/legal compliance, or (2) the franchisor provides a written analysis showing the franchisee can recover the cost and earn a positive return within a commercially reasonable period. This is a **direct, current, legislative attack on unjustified PIP mandates** — exactly the pain point this candidate targets. Sources: [A5050 bill text](https://pub.njleg.state.nj.us/Bills/2026/A5500/5050_I1.HTM), [S4202 bill text](https://pub.njleg.gov/Bills/2026/S4500/4202_I1.HTM).
  - A related, earlier version of NJ hotel-franchise legislation (A1958, focused more on loyalty points/vendor rebates than PIPs specifically) **passed the NJ General Assembly** (per [Hotel Dive coverage](https://www.hoteldive.com/news/new-jersey-hotel-bill-reactions/651664/), citing AAHOA support and AHLA/IFA opposition) but a companion Senate bill **stalled without a hearing** as of a **January 11, 2024** [Hotel Dive article](https://www.hoteldive.com/news/new-jersey-franchising-bill-stalls/704304/), with AHLA and the International Franchise Association actively lobbying against it. This shows the legislative fight is real but currently stalled/contested, not yet law.
  - **South Carolina**: "Franchise Act of 2026" ([SC Bill 1007](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/sess126_2025-2026/bills/1007.htm)) addressing franchisor "direct and immediate control" — currently in Senate Labor, Commerce and Industry Committee; general franchise-relations bill, not PIP-specific per what I found.
  - **Federal**: American Franchise Act (H.R.5267/S.3525, introduced Sept 10, 2025) — targets NLRA joint-employer standards, not PIPs directly. Hotel Fees Transparency Act (S.314/H.R.1479) — targets fee disclosure, not PIPs.
- **Conclusion:** There is a real, current (2025-2026), concrete legislative trend of franchisee associations and state legislators pushing back on unjustified/unprofitable brand-mandated capital expenditures — the NJ bill is the clearest, most recent, most on-point evidence. This validates that PIP cost fairness is a live political and financial pain point, not just an anecdotal owner complaint.

## Key Risks for This Candidate

1. **Direct, live, low-priced competition already exists.** PIPGURU ($25-$200/mo, 66+ brands, 200+ line items) is not a hypothetical competitor — it is a functioning product with pricing essentially identical to what a new entrant would likely propose. PIPGENIUS is a second well-positioned entrant already in private beta with a founding-client waitlist. Any new entrant needs a clear differentiation (e.g., broker/lender integration, owner-side negotiation support, financing bundling) beyond "cost estimate calculator," since that exact wedge is already served.
2. **The headline pain-point statistics offered in the brief are largely a decade old** (HREC 2016, Magnuson 2016). They are illustrative of a real, recurring dynamic (PIP scope/cost blowouts, single-item costs eating a large share of small-property revenue) but should not be marketed as current market data without fresh sourcing.
3. **The "PIP cycle compressing 10yr→7yr" statistic could not be verified anywhere** and may not be a real, attributable data point — do not use it without finding a named source.
4. **Reference cost guides (Nehmer/HVS, RLB) are paid, not free** — meaning the "authoritative free benchmark" gap this candidate might exploit is narrower than assumed; owners who want authoritative numbers already have a $ paid option, and cheap/free upstarts (PIPGURU) already undercut it.
5. **AAHOA's formal 12 Points do not explicitly name PIPs/capex** as a point, so this candidate cannot claim "leveraging an established advocacy framework"; the real current advocacy leverage is at the state-legislative level (NJ bill), which is a different mechanism (litigation/legislation) than a SaaS tool addresses.
6. **Direct-fetch failures**: CoStar (costar.com) and Hotel Management (hotelmanagement.net) both blocked automated fetches (HTTP 403) for the two most quote-critical claims (#1 HREC, #2 Hunter). Those two claims rest on search-engine-extracted snippets, not verified primary-source text — treat with slightly lower confidence than the directly-fetched claims (Magnuson, Getzler Henrich, PIPGURU, PIPGENIUS, AAHOA pages, NJ bill text).

## Pain Score: 6/10
Real, recurring, financially significant pain (PIP scope surprises, six-figure-to-seven-figure cost blowouts, legislative pushback) — but the most vivid supporting anecdotes are a decade old, and the CapEx backlog figure, while real, is industry-wide framing rather than direct evidence of individual-owner desperation for a benchmarking tool specifically.

## Willingness-to-Pay Score: 4/10
Two live competitors (PIPGURU at $25-$200/mo, PIPGENIUS in beta with waitlist pricing) demonstrate the market will pay *something*, but at low SaaS-style price points ($25-$200/mo), not high-value consulting fees — and the existence of established paid alternatives (Nehmer/HVS guide, consultants at $5,000-$15,000/estimate) means a new entrant is fighting for share of an already-served, price-compressed niche rather than capturing unmet willingness-to-pay.
