## Claim 1: HKA CRUX Insight — scope changes as largest dispute driver in Americas (~25.7%, ~31.1% cost overrun)

**Verdict: VERIFIED, and current (no newer edition supersedes it as of today).**

- HKA's **8th Annual CRUX Insight Report** ("From Insight to Foresight") was published **November 2025** (HKA's own page, https://www.hka.com/news/crux-insight-eighth-annual-report-from-insight-to-foresight/) and covers >2,200 projects in 114 countries, combined CapEx of $2.433T.
- Construction Dive's coverage (published **December 18, 2025**, https://www.constructiondive.com/news/time-money-fewer-construction-disputes-americas/808089/), explicitly attributed to "HKA's 2025 CRUX report" (i.e., this 8th edition), states: change-in-scope issues impacted **25.7%** of disputed projects in the Americas (vs. 38.8% globally), and Americas cost overruns claimed averaged **31.1%** of budget region-wide (with a further breakout that overruns on 2020+ projects moderated to 26.8%). This matches the memo's figures exactly.
- I searched specifically for a 9th annual edition or any newer 2026 CRUX release — **none exists yet**. HKA's CRUX report page (https://www.hka.com/crux-insight/crux-reports/) still lists the 8th annual as the latest as of this research date.
- Globally (not Americas-specific), the 8th edition shows scope change as dispute driver **declining** from >36% to ~28% year-over-year, and deficient workmanship in the Americas fell from 22% to 14% — so the overall disputes picture is improving, but scope-change-driven disputes remain the #1 named driver in the Americas specifically, and cost overrun magnitude (31.1%) is largely unchanged/consistent with the memo's characterization.
- **No red flags**: figures are properly sourced to the most recent (8th, Nov 2025) HKA report, well within the 2-year freshness window.

## Claim 2: Baker Tilly / Moss Adams sell "independent change order review" services

**Verdict: VERIFIED for both firms.**

- **Baker Tilly**: dedicated insight page "Auditing construction contract change orders" (https://www.bakertilly.com/insights/auditing-construction-contract-change-orders) explicitly recommends independent/third-party change order review — citing benefits of "improved project controls, enhanced transparency... real-time assessment of budget increases and schedule extensions... risk mitigation and early detection of potential errors, fraud, or mismanagement." Also has a live service line: "Construction audits and consulting" (https://www.bakertilly.com/services/consulting/strategy-and-operations/capital-projects-advisory/construction-audits) which performs "construction contract analyses, review payment applications, analyze change orders and contingency usage, and perform construction cost testing."
- **Moss Adams**: article "Managing Construction Change Orders: Reduce Risks and Improve Controls" (https://www.mossadams.com/articles/2025/10/managing-construction-change-orders, published Oct 2025 — recent) describes third-party change order review as "an independent assessment of project change orders submitted by contractors on a regular basis throughout the project life cycle," and Moss Adams' Program Management Support service explicitly "reviews contractor payment applications for overcharges, and evaluates change order cost submissions and validates contractor compliance."
- Both firms sell this as a professional-services offering (billed hours/consulting), not as software — this is consistent with the memo's characterization and confirms real, current demand for the underlying service that a software tool could partially disintermediate or augment.

## Claim 4: Akerman LLP hospitality guidance flags brand-directive-driven change orders

**Verdict: VERIFIED.**

- Akerman's article "Navigating Construction Contract Risks in Hotel Repositioning Projects" (https://www.akerman.com/en/perspectives/navigating-construction-contract-risks-in-hotel-repositioning-projects.html) explicitly states hotel projects "frequently face change orders due to late-stage brand directives altering finishes, furniture packages, or public space layouts," and recommends contract mechanisms (allowances, brand technical-services approval requirements) specifically to control this pattern.
- Also relevant/reinforcing: Akerman's market-data note that renovation/brand-conversion activity (259,495 rooms in Q2 2025) vastly outpaces new-build hotel construction (27,101 rooms same quarter) — meaning renovation-driven change-order risk is the larger and growing share of hotel construction activity, strengthening the market-size case.
- A second Akerman piece, "Protecting Hotel Brand Standards With Architect Agreements," reinforces that undocumented brand standards are a direct cause of change-order disputes.

## Claim 5: ClearStory (clearstory.build) — funding, scale, positioning

**Verdict: VERIFIED, with a MATERIAL UPDATE — the memo's figures are stale/outgrown.**

- **Funding**: Confirmed history — Seed $5.3M (Jun 2021), Series A $7M (Jun 2022), Series B $16M (Jun 2024, led by Prudence, w/ Industry Ventures, Jackson Square Ventures, Building Ventures, GS Futures, Cloud Apps Capital) — total disclosed ~$28.3M (ClearStory press room, https://www.clearstory.build/press-room). No Series C found as of this research.
- **Scale — the memo's numbers are now outdated.** The memo cites ~$2.1B/month change-order value and 14,000+ contractors. A more recent secondary source (Bricks & Bytes, "Clearstory's Real Asset Isn't the Software — It's 1.3 Million Change Orders," published **May 29, 2026**, https://bricks-bytes.com/technology/clearstory-change-order-data-moat/) reports updated figures from a podcast appearance: **~$3.5B/month** in change orders flowing through the platform and **~30,000 monthly active users** — i.e., ClearStory has grown roughly 65% in dollar volume and doubled its user base since the $2.1B/14,000 figures the memo used. **Flag: the memo is using stale numbers; ClearStory is scaling faster than the memo implies.** (Note: MAU ≠ "contractors," so the 14k→30k comparison is directionally indicative but not a precisely like-for-like metric; treat with some caution.)
- **Positioning — confirmed collaborative, not adversarial.** Both Construction Dive's Nov 17, 2025 piece on Turner's rollout (https://www.constructiondive.com/news/turner-clearstory-change-orders-technology/805717/ — Turner has used ClearStory on 350+ projects) and the Bricks & Bytes deep-dive describe ClearStory explicitly as a shared GC↔subcontractor workflow network ("bill.com for change orders"), where GCs often contractually mandate subs to submit through the platform. This is a **cooperative, multi-party workflow/communication tool**, not an owner-side adversarial audit/benchmarking tool.
- **Owner-side benchmarking**: I found **no evidence** ClearStory has added owner-side cost-benchmarking (i.e., "is this change order price above/below market") functionality. Their own press materials reference "Owner-Facing Change Order Logs" (visibility/transparency for owners) but nothing framed as benchmarking against market rates or flagging above-market pricing. This is a meaningful whitespace signal supporting the candidate's differentiation, **as of today** — but ClearStory's rapid scale growth (see above) means it is a fast-moving, well-capitalized incumbent that could plausibly add this feature.

## Claim 6: Procore, Buildertrend, Autodesk Construction Cloud — cost-benchmarking or backup-auditing (not just tracking)? Recent AI announcements?

**Verdict: No incumbent found with true change-order cost-benchmarking or backup/above-market-pricing auditing. All three remain workflow/tracking-oriented, though AI feature velocity is high and worth monitoring.**

- **Procore**: On **May 21, 2026**, Procore announced an expanded "Procore AI" experience embedding Datagrid's multimodal index, adding native agents for Deep Search, Submittals, RFI, Daily Log, and **Contract Review**, plus "Triggers" that let agents auto-respond to events including new change orders (Procore press release, https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260521183451/en/New-Procore-AI-Experience-Embeds-Datagrid-into-Procore; also covered by Engineering.com and Under the Hard Hat re: ConExpo 2026). This is a **critical fact to flag**: Procore is moving fast on AI-driven document/contract review, and a change-order-specific cost-benchmarking agent would be a very short logical next step for them given the Contract Review + Triggers infrastructure already shipped. I found **no evidence yet** that Procore has shipped market-rate benchmarking or above-market flagging specifically for change orders — but the infrastructure (AI contract review, event triggers) is now in place for them to build it.
- **Autodesk Construction Cloud (now merged into Autodesk Forma)**: January 2026 release notes show only workflow conveniences (Change Order deep-links in Budget view, streamlined Owner/Supplier Change Order creation) — no benchmarking. Autodesk's public 2026 AI-trends commentary discusses AI eventually handling "invoices, pay apps, change orders, lien waivers, and vendor compliance" by "end of 2026," but this is forward-looking/aspirational commentary, not a shipped benchmarking feature. **No dedicated cost-benchmarking feature for change orders found as of this research.**
- **Buildertrend**: 2026 features center on AI Client Updates (auto-drafted client-facing summaries pulling from Daily Logs/Change Orders/Invoices) and Smart Bill Capture (AP automation) — both are workflow/communication automation, not cost-benchmarking or auditing. Buildertrend is also primarily a residential/light-commercial platform, less relevant to hotel-scale renovation projects.
- **Bottom line**: as of July 2026, none of the three major incumbents has shipped a change-order cost-benchmarking or above-market-pricing-detection feature. Procore is the one to watch most closely given its AI Contract Review + Triggers launch — this materially raises platform risk for the candidate over a 12-24 month horizon even though today it's clear whitespace.

## Scores

**Pain score: 7/10** — Scope-change disputes are a well-documented, persistent pain point (25.7% of Americas disputes, 31.1% avg overrun per the newest HKA data), reinforced by professional-services firms actively selling manual review, and hotel-specific brand-directive dynamics (Akerman) make this particularly acute in hotel renovation. Not a 9-10 because the *broader* trend line (global scope-change disputes declining, workmanship disputes falling) suggests the industry is already improving change-order discipline somewhat, and much of the "reactive" pain is currently being absorbed (imperfectly) by existing consulting relationships.

**Willingness-to-pay score: 6/10** — Clear evidence that owners/GCs already pay real money for manual independent change-order review (Baker Tilly, Moss Adams, MDD-adjacent forensic work), which validates budget exists for this function. However: (1) no dedicated software product occupies this exact niche today, meaning WTP for a *software* solution (vs. a consulting engagement) is unproven; (2) a single hotel owner's renovation change-order volume may be too low-frequency to justify a recurring SaaS spend versus an as-needed consulting engagement; (3) well-capitalized adjacent platforms (Procore, ClearStory) have distribution and could bundle a benchmarking feature cheaply once built, compressing willingness to pay for a standalone tool.

## Key risks

- **Platform risk**: Procore's May 2026 AI Contract Review + Triggers launch shows the largest incumbent is actively building toward automated change-order intelligence; a benchmarking feature could ship from them within 12-24 months.
- **ClearStory's rapid growth** (~$2.1B→~$3.5B/month, ~14k→~30k users in roughly a year per available data points) shows the collaborative-workflow layer is consolidating fast; if ClearStory pivots toward owner-side analytics, its existing data network (1.3M+ change orders per Bricks & Bytes) would be a formidable moat advantage a new entrant lacks.
- **Frequency/ticket-size mismatch**: individual hotel owners renovate infrequently; the buyer may be better modeled as owner's reps, PE sponsors with renovation pipelines, or lenders — not single-asset owners — and the go-to-market/pricing model needs to reflect that rather than assuming standalone per-owner SaaS willingness to pay.
- **MDD's "change order audit" service framing is unconfirmed** — the reactive forensic-accounting comp may be weaker evidence of direct competitive/service-substitute risk than the memo implies.
- **Regional data limits**: verified HKA figures (25.7%/31.1%) are Americas-wide, not hotel-specific — no hotel-vertical-specific dispute statistics were found; the hotel-specific angle rests on the Akerman brand-directive pattern rather than quantified hotel dispute data.
