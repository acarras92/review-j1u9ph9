## 2. Edgewater Hotel (Madison, WI): $16.3M mechanics lien; lost $14M arbitration — CONFIRMED

**Verified facts:**
- **J.H. Findorff & Son filed a $16.3 million construction lien** against The Edgewater hotel project in Dane County Circuit Court. Source: madison.com, "J.H. Findorff & Son files $16.3 million construction lien against Edgewater hotel." At the time, this was one of **16 liens totaling $23.7 million** filed against the project by various contractors — the $16.3M was the largest single lien, not the total.
- **Arbitration outcome:** An American Arbitration Association panel, after 28 days of testimony (Feb 28–May 26 of the relevant year), ruled substantially against the hotel ownership entity (Hammes/Edgewater). The panel ordered:
  - The Edgewater/Hammes to pay **$13.8 million to Findorff** (close to, but not exactly, the "$14M" figure in the candidate framing — directionally correct, off by ~$0.2M) and $289,287 to Elkus/Manfredi Architects.
  - Findorff, in turn, was ordered to pay $1.4M to Nickles Electric and $3.1M to J.F. Ahern Co. (subs below Findorff) — i.e., a cascading liability structure.
  - The panel found Hammes/Edgewater **"did not properly manage The Edgewater hotel project,"** failed to provide timely design/construction documents, failed to negotiate change orders/time extensions in good faith, and failed to make timely payments.
- Source: https://madison.com/wsj/news/local/arbitration-panel-rules-against-the-edgewater-hotel-orders-million-payment/article_01e709a1-9a2b-565e-b120-9379b28dcdb2.html and https://madison.com/news/local/govt-and-politics/j-h-findorff-son-files-16-3-million-construction-lien-against-edgewater-hotel/article_46ea20e7-95e7-5b19-ac18-17b7f7b9fefe.html

**Dating flag:** This case is from the Edgewater's ~2012-2014 renovation era (article bylines/context and related lien articles from 2015 on the same domain: dailyreporter.com 2015 article on $9.6M in liens is part of the same saga). **This is old** (~11-14 years old as of 2026), well beyond the "~2 years" freshness bar — flag as **dated evidence**. It's also worth noting the arbitration finding actually cuts *against* the pure "GC track record" pitch angle: here it was the **owner/developer (Hammes)** who mismanaged the project and was found liable, not the GC (Findorff) who was largely vindicated and paid. This is a useful nuance: not every high-dollar construction dispute is "bad GC" — sometimes the dispute record would surface owner-side mismanagement instead, which is a double-edged sword for a product framed as "vet your GC."

**Verdict: Verified** (numbers reasonably accurate, both facts confirmed) but **dated** (~2013-2015 vintage) and directionally complicates the pitch's framing since the arbitration blamed the owner more than the contractor.

## 5. Feasibility probe: are underlying public records actually accessible at reasonable cost? — KEY FINDING, MIXED/CONCERNING FOR BUILDABILITY

This is the most important section for a buildability verdict.

### Court dockets
- **CourtListener / RECAP (Free Law Project):** **Free.** Confirmed via https://free.law/2026/05/07/api-included-in-memberships/ and wiki.free.law pages: "The CourtListener API is free to use without authentication at standard rate limits... no paywall on any document, no fee per page." Free tier rate limits are modest (5 req/min, 50/hr, 125/day as of a May 2026 update) but paid membership tiers unlock higher limits. **Caveat:** this covers *federal* court records (PACER-sourced via RECAP crowdsourcing) — coverage of documents not already in the RECAP archive requires the requester's own PACER account and PACER's per-page fees apply. **State court** dockets (where most mechanic's-lien and many construction-defect suits are filed, e.g., Philadelphia Court of Common Pleas, Dane County Circuit Court) are generally **not covered by CourtListener/RECAP at all** — that system is federal-only.
- **UniCourt:** Paid, tiered SaaS. Confirmed pricing from https://unicourt.com/pricing/: Personal $59/mo (10 searches, 1 user), Professional $199/mo (50 searches, 2 users), Premium $399/mo (200 searches, 5 users), Enterprise = custom. **No free trial** ("we do not offer free trials since most of our subscriptions are month-to-month"). API access is billed separately per-call for Search/Case/Document-download/Order-Document-Confirm/Update-Cases actions, with Enterprise custom API pricing. UniCourt does offer a free "CrowdSourced Library" of documents already downloaded by other users, but a systematic hotel-owner-facing product would need paid API access at volume, likely Enterprise-tier custom pricing (undisclosed, requires sales contact).
- **Trellis:** Also a paid legal-data subscription: Personal $69.95/mo, Research $129.95/mo, Research+Judge Analytics $199.95/mo (single-state coverage on these tiers); multi-state/API/Enterprise pricing requires contacting sales — not publicly disclosed. Source: https://trellis.law/plans (via search snippet).
- **State/county court dockets generally:** No unified free national source. Coverage is fragmented by county/state, with paid aggregators (UniCourt, Trellis) as the practical route to get anything close to systematic coverage, and cost scales with search/case volume — a real ongoing cost driver, not a one-time cost.

### Mechanic's-lien filings (county recorders)
- **Highly fragmented, county-by-county.** Search snippets (Levelset's per-county lien-filing pages, LegalClarity, county recorder FAQs) confirm most counties provide *some* free online index/search, but data quality, searchability, and historical depth vary widely. Copy costs are typically small (~$1-6/page for certified/plain copies; e.g., LA County $5-6/page, Montgomery County OH $2/page, Freestone County TX $1/page or flat monthly commercial-access fee).
- **No single national mechanic's-lien database exists.** Building a product that reliably surfaces liens nationwide would require either (a) integrating with a firm that already aggregates this (e.g., Levelset itself, which has "millions of construction projects" of lien data — but that's a would-be competitor's proprietary dataset) or (b) scraping/paying county-by-county, which is a materially large ongoing data-operations burden (thousands of counties, inconsistent formats, some still paper-only).

### Contractor licensing-board actions
- **Generally free and public**, but again fragmented per state. Confirmed examples: Nevada State Contractors Board has a public disciplinary-actions search (https://www.nvcontractorsboard.com/investigations/disciplinary-actions/, https://app.nvcontractorsboard.com/...DisciplineSearchPage.aspx); California CSLB has a free public license-check tool with complaint disclosure (https://www.cslb.ca.gov/onlineservices/checklicenseII/checklicense.aspx); Pennsylvania publishes a monthly disciplinary-actions listing (https://www.pa.gov/agencies/dos/alerts-and-notices/professional-licensing-disciplinary-actions). Aggregating this nationally (~50 states + territories, each with its own board, format, and update cadence) is feasible but labor-intensive to build and maintain.

### Bottom line on feasibility
- **Federal court records:** cheap/free via CourtListener but largely irrelevant (most construction litigation is in state court).
- **State/county court dockets:** the highest-value source for this product idea, and the **most costly/least standardized** — realistic ongoing costs likely run from low hundreds to low thousands of dollars/month depending on volume via UniCourt/Trellis, plus the need to still manually check counties/states those aggregators don't cover well, or pay per-document from PACER-style sources with no federal equivalent at state level.
- **Mechanic's liens:** free-to-cheap per-document but **no aggregator**, meaning a real product would need to build a 50-state/county scraping-and-refresh pipeline — this is a substantial engineering/data-ops lift, arguably the single biggest technical risk to the concept.
- **Licensing board actions:** free but fragmented; buildable with real (non-trivial) integration effort across states.
- **Overall: buildable, but not cheap or simple.** This is a real, non-trivial data-aggregation business (similar in kind to what background-check/compliance vendors like Business Screen already do — see Section 7), not a thin wrapper on one clean API. Total cost of goods sold for a credible national product is meaningfully above zero and scales with coverage breadth.

## 6. Defamation / legal-risk landscape for publishing GC litigation histories

**Verified facts:**
- **Fair-report privilege** is a real and well-established defense (confirmed via Digital Media Law Project, First Amendment Encyclopedia, and law-firm blog sources) that protects **"complete, unbiased, and accurate"** reporting on official proceedings, including court filings/records, from defamation liability — "the privilege permits the publication of false and defamatory statements uttered in judicial and other official public proceedings" as long as the report is a fair and accurate summary and properly attributed to the official source.
- **Important caveats found:**
  - **Not all states recognize the privilege**, and among those that do, scope varies significantly — some states extend it broadly to virtually all government records, others interpret it narrowly (confirmed: New Jersey Supreme Court applied it to civil filings; separate New Jersey and Kentucky decisions have narrowed it in other contexts, per Brooks Pierce law firm blog posts).
  - The privilege requires **accuracy and fair attribution** — selective, misleading, or one-sided quotation, even from real court records, can void the privilege ("selective quotation appearing maliciously" is treated differently than "honest mistakes" in condensing).
  - Fair-report privilege is a **state common-law/first-amendment doctrine**, distinct from **Section 230** (which protects platforms from liability for *user-generated* content, not a publisher's own editorial summaries of court records). A product that itself writes/summarizes "Contractor X lost a $174.6M judgment for defective work" is **acting as a publisher of its own content**, not hosting third-party user content — so Section 230 would likely **not** apply to protect the product's own editorial characterizations; the product would need to rely on fair-report privilege (and truth/accuracy as the ultimate defense) instead.
  - **Practical risk:** an appeal pending (as in the Tutor Perini case) means characterizing a judgment as "final" or omitting the pending-appeal status could itself create liability exposure or at least a fairness/accuracy problem — the product would need rigorous "status: on appeal" labeling discipline.
- Sources: https://www.dmlp.org/legal-guide/fair-report-privilege, https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/article/fair-report-privilege/, https://www.brookspierce.com/digital-media-and-data-privacy-law-blog/N-J-Supreme-Court-Applies-Fair-Report-Privilege-to-Civil-Filings, https://www.brookspierce.com/digital-media-and-data-privacy-law-blog/New-Jersey-and-Kentucky-Decisions-Narrow-Fair-Report-Privilege

**Verdict:** Fair-report privilege is real and provides meaningful (not absolute) protection when the product limits itself to accurate, attributed summaries of actual court/lien/licensing-board records. Section 230 is **not** the relevant shield for a product's own editorial content (as opposed to hosted user reviews) — this is a nuance the candidate's framing should not conflate. Legal risk is manageable but requires real editorial rigor (attribution, accuracy, appeal-status tracking), likely legal review, and probably media-liability insurance — a real cost, not a blocker, but not "no legal risk" either.

## 7. Existing commercial-construction contractor-vetting products — CONFIRMED, MEANINGFUL COMPETITION EXISTS

This is a critical finding: **a direct competitor already exists and covers close to the exact feature set proposed.**

- **Business Screen** (businessscreen.com) — confirmed via direct fetch of https://www.businessscreen.com/use-cases/construction-commercial-real-estate — explicitly offers "Construction & Real Estate Due Diligence" screening for **subcontractors and principals** covering: professional license verification, **mechanic's liens from prior projects**, OSHA violations/safety records, insurance certs, **civil records and litigation history**, criminal records on principals, corporate standing, **adverse media and regulatory actions**, tax/judgment liens, and financial distress indicators. It offers three tiers (Preliminary/automated, Advanced/investigator-sourced with direct jurisdiction searches, Deep Dive/20-year lookback across multiple jurisdictions and name variations). It explicitly names the exact pain point the candidate is targeting: *"mechanic's liens, active litigation, or financial distress from prior projects rarely shows up in a self-reported application."* Pricing is not disclosed publicly (requires contacting sales), and it appears aimed more broadly at subcontractor/vendor vetting than specifically at hotel-owner GC selection, but functionally this is the same product category.
- **Surety/insurer-side tools:** Confirmed via CFMA (Construction Financial Management Association) sources that sureties already prequalify contractors using the "3 Cs" (capacity, capital, character) and that a healthy surety relationship is itself treated as a track-record signal ("a healthy, established surety relationship signals that an underwriter has already vetted the firm's finances and track record" — from search synthesis of vetting-guide content). Named prequalification software platforms in active use: **PreQual, Procore, COMPASS (1Form-based, used by ~92% of surety applications per search result), TradeTapp, Highwire, Oracle Textura, VelocityEHS, Constrafor** — these focus heavily on financial statements, bonding capacity, safety records, and D&B reports rather than litigation/lien history specifically, but COMPASS's "Q Score" explicitly folds in safety records and "business characteristics" into a risk profile that surety underwriters already rely on. Source: https://cfma.org/articles/mitigating-risk-and-uncertainty-surety-prequalification-for-contractors, https://www.vertikalrms.com/article/best-subcontractor-prequalification-software-2026-top-8/ (search snippets).
- Net: the "no one aggregates this for hotel owners" premise is **weakened** — Business Screen already does close to this exact thing (litigation + liens + licensing + OSHA + adverse media, tiered investigator-augmented reports) as a general commercial-construction/real-estate due diligence product, and the surety industry has its own mature prequalification ecosystem (though oriented more toward financial/bonding capacity than litigation narrative). A hotel-owner-specific product would need to differentiate on price, hotel-vertical packaging/workflow, or speed/self-serve UX rather than claim to be filling an empty niche.

## Scoring

**Pain score: 5/10.** The underlying pain is real and the headline case (Tutor Perini, $174.6M + $42.4M, live and current) is a genuinely dramatic, verifiable data point that shows GC failures can be catastrophic for hotel owners. But the pain is not undifferentiated from general commercial-construction owner risk — it's not hotel-specific, and sophisticated owners already lean on surety relationships, references, and (increasingly) products like Business Screen to get at this. The Edgewater case actually illustrates the pain cuts both ways (owner mismanagement, not just GC malfeasance), complicating the pure "vet your GC" pitch.

**Willingness-to-pay score: 4/10.** Hotel owners undertaking $50M+ construction projects clearly have budget for diligence (they already pay for legal, surety, insurance, and increasingly commercial background-check services). But: (a) a well-funded, tiered incumbent (Business Screen) already sells this exact capability with investigator augmentation, meaning a new entrant must compete on price/speed/vertical UX, not on the existence of the data; (b) the underlying data costs are real and non-trivial (state court/lien aggregation is genuinely hard and costly to build/maintain), squeezing margins; (c) large, sophisticated hotel developers/owners already have relationships with sureties and legal counsel who informally provide much of this signal for a much larger project already in motion, reducing incremental willingness to pay for a standalone lookup tool versus bundling it into an existing risk/legal workflow.

## Key Risks
1. Direct, already-operating competitor (Business Screen) offers a near-identical multi-source screening product (litigation, liens, licensing, OSHA, adverse media) for commercial construction — the "nobody aggregates this" premise is false.
2. Core data sourcing (state/county court dockets, mechanic's liens) has no clean national aggregator; either cost passes through from paid legal-data vendors (UniCourt/Trellis, tiered $59-$400+/mo retail, Enterprise custom for volume) or requires building/maintaining a 50-state, thousands-of-county scraping pipeline — a real, ongoing engineering/data-ops cost center, not a one-time build.
3. Defamation/fair-report-privilege protection is real but conditional (accuracy, attribution, not-all-states-recognize-it) and Section 230 does not cover the product's own editorial summaries — appeal-status tracking and legal review are non-optional, ongoing costs.
4. The most dramatic anchor case (Tutor Perini) is still on appeal — any product citing it as a settled fact of GC wrongdoing risks both inaccuracy and defamation exposure if the appellate outcome differs.
5. The Edgewater case shows litigation-history products can just as easily surface owner-side failures as GC-side failures, which may undercut a pitch narrowly framed as "protect yourself from bad GCs."
6. Sophisticated hotel owners already have substitute signals (surety relationships/bonding capacity, references, informal industry reputation) that reduce incremental willingness to pay for a stand-alone lookup product.
