### 1.3 Brand renovation-cycle frequency

- Industry-standard cadence per multiple hospitality-construction and brokerage sources: **soft-goods renovation ~every 6-7 years, hard/case-goods renovation ~every 12-14 years, full repositioning ~every 18 years.** Sources: [King Construction, "Hotel PIP Fulfillment"](https://www.kingconstruction-usa.com/blog-articles/hotel-property-improvement-plan); [GLR Inc., "What is a Hotel PIP Really?"](https://glrinc.net/hospitality/what-is-a-hotel-property-improvement-plan-pip/).
- Broader framing: PIPs are described as recurring **"every 5-7 years,"** or more conservatively **"every 7-15 years,"** with earlier triggers on brand-standard failure, low guest-satisfaction scores, or (most relevant to this product) **change of ownership** — a sale almost always triggers a fresh PIP review regardless of where the property sits in its renovation cycle. Sources: same King Construction / GLR links above; change-of-ownership trigger corroborated by [The Plasencia Group, "Slaying the PIP Dragon: Navigating Brand Mandates While Selling A Hotel"](https://tpghotels.com/slaying-the-pip-dragon-navigating-brand-mandates-while-selling-a-hotel/) and [HVS, "The Current Impact of Brand PIP Requirements on Hotel Sales"](https://www.hvs.com/article/8642-the-current-impact-of-brand-pip-requirements-on-hotel-sales).
- Cost-as-share-of-revenue framing (useful for market sizing, not per-PIP counting): hoteliers should budget **~7-8% of revenue over a 10-year period** for PIP costs. Source: [Matthews, "Things to Consider if Your Hotel PIP Is Coming Due"](https://www.matthews.com/market_insights/things-to-consider-if-your-hotel-pip-is-coming-due) (also syndicated via [HFTP](https://www.hftp.org/news/4121838/things-to-consider-if-your-hotel-pip-is-coming-due) and [Hospitality Net](https://www.hospitalitynet.org/opinion/4121838/things-to-consider-if-your-hotel-pip-is-coming-due)).

### 1.4 Estimate: how many PIPs are issued per year in the U.S.

No source — free or paid-search-accessible — publishes a direct annual PIP count. Building a transparent, sourced **[estimated]** range from the above:

**Method A — renovation-cycle-driven (organic/scheduled PIPs):**
- Base: ~36,000 franchised hotels (AHLA figure, §1.1).
- Assume a blended average renovation-trigger interval of ~7 years (soft-goods cycle, the most frequent recurring PIP type per §1.3 sources).
- 36,000 ÷ 7 ≈ **~5,100 scheduled/cyclical PIPs per year.** **[estimated]** — sensitivity: at a 10-year blended cycle, ~3,600/year; at a 6-year cycle, ~6,000/year.

**Method B — transaction-driven (change-of-ownership PIPs):**
- LWHA counted 392 tracked deals ≥$10M in 2025 (§1.2), each of which typically triggers a PIP review per Plasencia Group/HVS sourcing above.
- Because LWHA's threshold excludes the bulk of small select-service/midscale deals (the core ICP), true total annual U.S. hotel changes-of-ownership are materially higher than 392 — but I found **no free/public source with a total transaction *count* (all price points) for U.S. hotels.** **[unclear]** — this is the single biggest quantification gap in this section. If a rough multiple of 5-8x the ≥$10M count is assumed for all-price-point deals (reasoning: small select-service assets trade far more frequently and at lower price points than the $10M+ tracked segment, per general CRE deal-size distribution logic — **[estimated]**, not sourced to a specific study), that implies **~2,000-3,100 change-of-ownership PIP triggers/year.** This multiplier is a placeholder assumption, not a cited figure, and should be labeled as such in any external communication.

**Combined estimate:** Adding Method A (cyclical, ~3,600-6,000) and Method B (transactional, ~2,000-3,100, with the caveat that some transactional PIPs coincide with a cyclical PIP already due) yields a working range of **roughly 5,000-9,000 PIPs issued per year in the U.S., with ~6,000-7,000 as a reasonable midpoint. This entire range is [estimated]** from the component figures above — it is a derived planning number for a founder, not a citable industry statistic, and should never be presented externally as sourced fact without this derivation attached.

## 2. ICP

### 2.1 Primary: Independent franchisee owner, 1-5 properties, select-service/midscale

- **Sizing evidence for this segment existing at scale:** AAHOA's ~20,000 members collectively own >36,000 hotels — an average of **~1.8 hotels per member**, strongly suggesting the median AAHOA member owns 1-3 properties rather than a large portfolio. Source: [AAHOA About page](https://www.aahoa.com/about/about-aahoa) (36,000+ hotels) and [Asian Hospitality](https://www.asianhospitality.com/study-aahoa-members-own-60-percent-of-u-s-hotels/) (60% ownership share, 20,000 members). **[estimated]** — the 1.8x average is my calculation from these two AAHOA-sourced figures, not a directly published AAHOA statistic; I could not find a published AAHOA breakdown of members by portfolio-size bucket (e.g., % owning exactly 1 hotel) — **[unclear]**.
- **Why this segment feels the pain most:**
  - They cannot spread a $500K-$2M PIP mandate across a large portfolio's balance sheet or a professional owner's asset-management team; a single PIP is often the largest capital event they'll face in the loan term.
  - They typically cannot afford (or don't know how to hire) HVS/Nehmer-caliber cost consultants, whose PIP estimate itself runs **$5,000-$15,000 and takes 2-3 weeks** — a real hurdle before the deal even pencils. Source: [PIPGURU homepage claims, per WebFetch extraction, thepipguru.com](https://www.thepipguru.com/); corroborated independently by [PIPGENIUS homepage](https://www.thepipgenius.com/), which cites the same "5-figure consultant fee, 2-3 week turnaround" pain point.
  - Select-service/limited-service assets are exactly the segment flagged as most operationally exposed in the current distress cycle: **15-20% of limited-service loans show distress**, concentrated among "smaller franchised assets in suburban markets that lack pricing power." Source: [Matthews, "The Rising Tide of Hotel Delinquency"](https://www.matthews.com/insights/hotel-delinquency).
  - Franchise agreements make PIP compliance mandatory — non-compliance risks termination of the franchise, which for a 1-5 property independent owner can mean losing the business entirely, not just one asset in a diversified portfolio. Source: [King Construction](https://www.kingconstruction-usa.com/blog-articles/hotel-property-improvement-plan); [Amerail Systems, "Navigating a PIP as a New Hotel Owner"](https://www.amerailsys.com/post/navigating-a-pip-as-a-new-hotel-owner).
  - **Can they buy on a credit card?** Plausibly yes for a self-serve, low-price product: PIPGURU's own live pricing (see §3 below and competitor analysis) is $25 one-time up to $200/month — squarely credit-card, no-PO, no-procurement-process territory for an owner-operator who personally signs checks. This is inference from observed competitor pricing structure, not a direct claim from any source about this ICP's purchasing behavior — **[estimated]**.

### 2.2 Secondary: small management/ownership groups, brokers, lenders

- **Small management/ownership groups (6-30 properties):** Feel the same pain as the primary ICP but with more internal capacity to absorb it (an asset-management or VP-ops function may exist). Likely a **higher-ACV, lower-volume** customer — could plausibly pay for the "Professional"/"Enterprise" tier equivalents (multi-seat, portfolio comparison) seen in competitor pricing. No direct source quantifies this segment's population size; **[unclear]**.
- **Hotel brokers:** Multiple deals reviewed show brokers directly navigating PIP issues in listings — e.g., a Marcus & Millichap-brokered Michigan sale where "the buyer plans to complete the remaining property improvement plan items and continue operating the hotel as a Fairfield Inn & Suites." Source: [Hotel Management, "Marcus & Millichap helps broker two transactions in Montana, Michigan"](https://www.hotelmanagement.net/transactions/marcus-millichap-helps-broker-two-transactions-montana-michigan). The Plasencia Group has published thought leadership specifically on PIPs as a deal-killer/deal-shaper in brokered sales: [tpghotels.com, "Slaying the PIP Dragon"](https://tpghotels.com/slaying-the-pip-dragon-navigating-brand-mandates-while-selling-a-hotel/). Brokers are a **channel** more than a buyer (they'd use the tool to keep deals alive / justify price adjustments to both sides), but could also be a paid-referral or white-label channel. **[unclear]** whether brokers would pay directly vs. refer.
- **Lenders requiring PIP reserves:** CMBS and bank lenders structurally require PIP capital to be escrowed at closing (brand-mandated PIP capital + 4-5% of gross revenue FF&E reserve), released only against verified contractor progress and architect certification, typically over a 12-18 month draw schedule. Sources: [PeerSense, "Hotel PIP Financing 2026"](https://peersense.com/hotel-pip-financing); [PeerSense, "Best Hotel Loan Lenders 2026"](https://peersense.com/best-hotel-lenders-2026). This means lenders have a direct, recurring institutional need for a credible independent PIP cost range at underwriting — a plausible B2B2B channel/upsell (e.g., a lender requires the borrower to produce a benchmarked estimate) rather than a primary buyer. **[estimated]** — no source describes lenders as a direct customer of any existing PIP-estimate product; this is inference from the escrow-mechanics sourcing.

### 2.3 Competitive context (per task background)

Two pre-existing tools were named in the brief as pre-revenue waitlist pages. Live research (2026-07-18) shows both now have **actual pricing published**, which changes the "pre-revenue waitlist" framing and should inform differentiation:
- **PIPGURU** (thepipguru.com): $25 one-time single estimate; $50/mo Starter (unlimited Gut Checks, 5 Deep Dives); $100/mo Professional; $200/mo Enterprise (5 seats, portfolio comparison). Claims 66+ brands, 200+ cost-guide line items, 2025 data, "hundreds of renovations" underlying the database, one asset-manager testimonial. Still references an "early access" waitlist with first-year-free incentive for beta testers. Source: WebFetch extraction of [thepipguru.com](https://www.thepipguru.com/), 2026-07-18.
- **PIPGENIUS** (thepipgenius.com): Explicitly labeled **"Private Beta"** with "Founding clients onboard May 2026." Tiers: Snapshot (24hr, deal screening), Blueprint (48hr, underwriting, currently available), Playbook (72hr, restructuring/capital planning, "coming soon"). Claims 80% cheaper than a consultant, 66+ brands auto-classified, 200+ line items, sub-10% confidence ranges, NDA-protected, hand-reviewed by analysts. No testimonials/independent verification found on-page. Source: WebFetch extraction of [thepipgenius.com](https://www.thepipgenius.com/), 2026-07-18.
- **Implication:** Both competitors emphasize *estimate speed and cost vs. consultants*. Neither, per their own homepage copy as extracted, foregrounds the specific angle in this product's brief — **benchmarking a brand's specific bid line-by-line against market ranges and generating a negotiation/pushback memo** for the owner to use against the brand/PM. That negotiation-memo output is a differentiation opening not addressed by either competitor's stated positioning.

## 4. 2026 CMBS-Maturity / PIP-Distress Concentration

- **Scale of the 2026 CMBS wall:** More than **$100 billion** in fixed- and floating-rate CMBS loans mature in 2026; one source puts the full 2026 CMBS maturity calendar at **$146.2B total, including $76.6B in "hard" maturities**. More than half of maturing loans are projected not to repay at maturity. Source: [DBRS Morningstar, "U.S. CRE 2026 Outlook"](https://dbrs.morningstar.com/research/471472); [CRE Daily, "CMBS Maturity Wall Tests Refinancing in 2026"](https://www.credaily.com/briefs/cmbs-maturity-wall-tests-refinancing-in-2026/).
- **Segment concentration — full-service is worse, but limited/select-service is where the ICP sits:**
  - **Full-service hotels:** ~**40-45%** of full-service CMBS loans flagged "potentially troubled," "troubled," or already transferred to a special servicer — the more acute distress pocket, but this segment is largely *outside* the 1-5 property independent-owner ICP (full-service assets are typically owned by larger/institutional groups).
  - **Limited/select-service hotels (the ICP's segment):** **~15-20%** distress — lower than full-service in percentage terms, but described as **"deeply structural and operational, concentrated among smaller franchised assets in suburban markets that lack pricing power and have absorbed operating cost inflation."** This is precisely the primary-ICP profile (small, franchised, limited-service, price-taker). Source: [Matthews, "The Rising Tide of Hotel Delinquency"](https://www.matthews.com/insights/hotel-delinquency).
  - **~70% of hotel loans carry floating-rate debt** originated in the 2020-2022 low-rate window, now facing refinancing into a 6-8% fixed-rate environment or forced sale once extension options are exhausted. Source: same [Matthews](https://www.matthews.com/insights/hotel-delinquency) analysis.
  - Distressed, flagged hotels are trading at **8.0-8.5% cap rates**, up from 6.5-7.5% in 2021-2022 — meaning more of these assets are or will be transacting, each such transaction being a probable fresh-PIP trigger event per §1.3/§2. Source: same [Matthews](https://www.matthews.com/insights/hotel-delinquency) piece.
- **Targeting implication:** The highest-intent, most-distressed near-term buyer segment is **small, suburban, franchised, limited/select-service hotels carrying floating-rate CMBS debt originated 2020-2022 and maturing in 2026**, where the owner must either refinance (triggering a lender-mandated PIP-reserve underwriting exercise, §2.2) or sell (triggering a change-of-ownership PIP review, §1.3/§2.1). This is exactly the primary ICP defined in §2.1, and the CMBS wall gives this a concrete, dated urgency window (2026) to anchor outbound/marketing messaging and timing.
